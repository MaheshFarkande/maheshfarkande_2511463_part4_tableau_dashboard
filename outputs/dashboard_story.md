# Dashboard Story — Executive Sales Dashboard

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The business is **profitable but plateaued**. Across two years, monthly revenue oscillates in a tight band — roughly ₹6.3M to ₹10.9M — without establishing a durable upward trend. Underneath that flat top line sits a clear story the panels tell together: **Technology drives the rupees, Office Supplies and Home Office drive the margins, and a handful of states carry the revenue.** The leadership question is therefore not "how do we sell more?" but "how do we convert a strong, volume-rich book into more reliable profit and steadier growth?" The levers are visible on this dashboard: protect the high-margin pockets, fix the volatile demand pattern, and address shipping and concentration risk before they cost us.

## 2. What Is Performing Well

The strongest thread runs from the **category chart** to the **segment table**. Technology — Copiers, Accessories, Phones — produces the largest absolute profit, so it is the revenue engine. But the segment table shows where profit is *most efficient*: Furniture sold to **Home Office** customers reaches the highest margins on the board (East peaks at 0.126), and Office Supplies sub-categories (Labels, Art, Storage, Binders) hold consistently strong margins despite small ticket sizes. Read together, these say the company has both a volume engine (Technology) and an efficiency engine (Office Supplies + Home Office) — a healthy combination most businesses lack. Geographically, the **map** confirms a solid revenue base, with leading states clearing ₹12–15M each.

## 3. What Is Underperforming

The **trend line** is the clearest underperformer: revenue ends the period (₹9.10M) essentially where it began (₹9.45M), with violent month-to-month swings in between. That instability undercuts the otherwise strong category and segment results. The **segment table** exposes a second soft spot — **Technology's margins are thin** (many cells 0.01–0.06) even though its volume is high, meaning our biggest seller is also one of our least efficient. And the **Consumer** segment, likely our largest by order count, shows lighter (lower-margin) cells than Home Office across most categories, suggesting we work hardest for our least profitable customers.

## 4. What Risks Are Visible

Three risks emerge when the panels are read against each other. **Revenue concentration:** the map shows top states out-earning the weakest by 70–90%, so a shock in one or two states would hit the whole top line. **Demand volatility:** the ~72% peak-to-trough swing in the trend line makes forecasting, staffing, and inventory unreliable. **Service risk:** Ship Mode Performance shows Standard Class averaging ~4.5 delivery days — far slower than other modes — yet its profit margin is no better, so a large block of customers waits longest for no financial upside, a classic precursor to dissatisfaction and returns.

## 5. What Opportunities Are Visible

The opportunities are the mirror image of the risks. **Mix-shift to margin:** because Office Supplies and Home Office already convert sales to profit efficiently, deliberately growing their share would lift blended profitability without needing more total volume. **Technology margin recovery:** Technology's high volume means even a small margin improvement (pricing, cost, or discount discipline) translates into large rupee gains. **Geographic expansion:** the lagging states on the map are either under-penetrated (a growth runway) or structurally weak (a place to stop spending) — and distinguishing the two is cheap to test. **Service-led retention:** fixing Standard Class speed could protect the revenue currently exposed to slow delivery.

## 6. Recommended Business Actions

1. **Run a margin-mix program.** Set targets to grow Office Supplies and Home Office share of the book, since the dashboard already proves they are the most efficient cells.
2. **Launch a Technology margin review.** Audit pricing, cost, and discounting on Copiers/Phones/Accessories — small gains here move the largest rupee totals.
3. **Smooth demand.** Map the trend dips against promotions and campaigns to determine whether discounting is pulling demand forward and creating the troughs; if so, redesign the promo calendar.
4. **Fix Standard Class.** Investigate the delivery-time lag and either speed it up or migrate customers to better modes, since the slow mode buys no extra margin.
5. **De-risk geography.** Defend the top states and pilot targeted growth in 2–3 lagging ones.

## 7. Limitations of the Dashboard

This dashboard supports diagnosis but not full root-cause analysis, for several reasons. It shows **no discount-vs-profit view**, so the link between discounting and the demand dips is inferred, not proven. It shows **no return analysis**, so return risk by category, segment, or ship mode is invisible here. The **segment table is labeled "Profit" but displays margin ratios** (e.g. 0.126), which can mislead a reader expecting rupee amounts. The **currency is assumed to be INR**. And the dashboard reflects *recorded* orders only — it cannot show lost demand, stockouts, or customer sentiment behind the volatility.

## 8. Suggested Next Analysis

To turn this diagnosis into decisions, three follow-ups matter most. First, a **Discount-vs-Profit scatter with a trend line** to find the discount threshold where orders cross into loss — directly informing Action 3. Second, a **Return Rate breakdown** by category, segment, and ship mode, tested against delivery speed, to confirm whether slow Standard Class is generating returns. Third, a **customer-level profitability and retention view** to validate whether Home Office's margin advantage holds over repeat business, and whether Consumer can be made more profitable rather than simply larger. Together these would convert the current "what is happening" picture into a defensible "what we should do and why."
